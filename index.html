The Intelligent Investor
Benjamin Graham

The definitive book on value investing strategy and long-term success.

Value Investing 15 min summary

Benjamin Graham's "The Intelligent Investor" is widely regarded as the definitive text on value investing. First published in 1949, its principles remain remarkably relevant in today's market.

Key Concepts:

  • The difference between investing and speculating
  • Mr. Market metaphor for understanding market psychology
  • Margin of Safety principle for reducing investment risk
  • Defensive vs. enterprising investor strategies
  • How to analyze financial statements to find undervalued companies

Warren Buffett, Graham's most famous student, has called it "the best book on investing ever written." The book emphasizes long-term strategies rather than get-rich-quick formulas.

Technical Analysis

Overview

Technical analysis is a trading discipline that uses historical price charts and trading data to predict future price movements. It involves examining patterns, trends (like support and resistance levels), and signals from various indicators (e.g., RSI, moving averages) to make informed buy or sell decisions.

Technicians believe that past market behavior, reflected in price and volume, can provide valuable insights into future performance because market psychology tends to repeat itself.

Overview

Fundamental traders often look for opportunities around earnings reports, analyst ratings, stock splits, and M&A activity, typically with a longer-term perspective than technical traders.

Understanding Market Cycles

Market cycles, also known as stock market cycles, refer to patterns or trends that emerge in different market or business environments. During these cycles, certain securities or asset classes may outperform others as their business models align with growth conditions. Understanding these cycles is crucial for making informed investment decisions.

It's important to note that pinpointing the exact current phase of a market cycle can be challenging, but they are generally characterized by four distinct phases:

The Four Phases of Market Cycles

  • Accumulation Phase: This phase occurs after a market has bottomed out. Sentiment is generally still negative, but innovators and early adopters see opportunity and begin to buy, anticipating the end of the decline.
  • Mark-up Phase (Uptrend): As market sentiment improves and more investors enter, prices begin to rise steadily. This phase is characterized by increasing trading volume and positive technical indicators.
  • Distribution Phase: After a significant uptrend, the asset price reaches a peak. Sellers begin to dominate as informed investors start taking profits, distributing their holdings to less experienced buyers who might be entering late.
  • Mark-down Phase (Downtrend): Once demand is exhausted and selling pressure takes over, the price starts to decline. This phase is characterized by falling prices and negative sentiment until the cycle eventually bottoms out, potentially leading to a new accumulation phase.

Support & Resistance Explained

Support and resistance are critical concepts in technical analysis. They represent price levels on a chart where the prevailing trend may pause or reverse due to supply and demand dynamics. Mastering these concepts can help traders identify strategic entry and exit points.

Understanding Support

Support is a price level where a downtrend is expected to pause due to a concentration of demand. Think of it as a 'floor' below the current price. When an asset's price falls towards a support level, it indicates that buying interest is increasing, which can halt the price decline and potentially lead to a reversal or consolidation.

  • Support levels are typically found at previous lows.
  • Increased buying volume at a support level can confirm its strength.
  • A break below a support level may indicate that sellers have overcome buyers, and the price could continue to fall.

Understanding Resistance

Resistance is the opposite of support. It's a price level where an uptrend is expected to pause due to a concentration of supply. Think of it as a 'ceiling' above the current price. As an asset's price rises towards a resistance level, selling interest typically increases, which can halt the price advance and potentially lead to a reversal or consolidation.

  • Resistance levels are typically found at previous highs.
  • Increased selling volume at a resistance level can confirm its strength.
  • A break above a resistance level may indicate that buyers have overcome sellers, and the price could continue to rise.

Traders use support and resistance to make decisions: buying near support in an uptrend and selling near resistance in a downtrend. If a support level is broken, it may become a new resistance level, and conversely, if a resistance level is broken, it may become new support.

Reading Financial Statements

Understanding a company's financial statements is crucial for any investor. These documents provide a scorecard of a company's financial health and performance. The three main statements offer different perspectives:

Key Financial Statements

  • Balance Sheet: This statement offers a snapshot of a company's assets (what it owns), liabilities (what it owes), and shareholders' equity at a specific point in time. It follows the fundamental equation: Assets = Liabilities + Equity. Think of it as a statement of net worth for the company.
  • Income Statement (Profit & Loss): This statement shows a company's financial performance over a specific period (e.g., a quarter or a year). It details revenues (or sales), the costs to achieve those revenues (cost of goods sold, operating expenses), and ultimately, the company's net profit or loss.
  • Cash Flow Statement: This statement tracks all the cash inflows and outflows within a company over a period. It's broken down into three main activities: operating (day-to-day business), investing (purchase or sale of assets like property or equipment), and financing (activities involving debt, equity, and dividends). It helps assess a company's ability to generate cash and meet its obligations.

While these statements are vital, remember to also read the accompanying notes to the financial statements, as they provide important context and details about the accounting methods used. Understanding the business itself and non-financial factors is also key to a comprehensive analysis.
